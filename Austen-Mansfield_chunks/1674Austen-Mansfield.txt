He had meant them to be good, but his cares had been directed to the understanding and manners, not the disposition. and of the necessity of selfdenial and humility, he feared they had never heard from any lips that could profit them. Bitterly did he deplore a deficiency which now he could scarcely comprehend to have been possible. Wretchedly did he feel, that with all the cost and care of an anxious and expensive education, he had brought up his daughters without their understanding their first duties, or his being acquainted with their character and temper. The high spirit and strong passions of Rushworth, especially, were made known to him only in their sad result.
